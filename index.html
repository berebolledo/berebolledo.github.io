Mitochondrial genomics, and bioinformatics lab


Hi! My name is Boris Rebolledo-Jaramillo, and I am an Assistant professor at The Institute for Science and Innovation in Medicine (ICIM), Universidad del Desarrollo, Chile.
I got a Ph.D. in Bioinformatics and Genomics from The Pennsylvania State University, under the direction of Anton Nekrutenko and Kateryna Makova , whom ignited my interest for mitochondrial genomics, and motivated me to focus on generating reproducible bioinformatics research.
Currently, I am in charge of our bioinformatics core facility at ICIM, and my research focuses on:
  • The contribution of mito-nuclear interactions to disease.
  • The development of bioinformatic pipelines for the discovery of causal genetic variants in rare undiagnosed disorders.

    My research: Mitochondrial genomics

  • I spent my doctoral training developing a bioinformatics pipeline for the unequivocal identification of mtDNA heteroplasmic sites from sequencing data. Back then, the sequencing error rates were higher, so the identification of true low level heteroplasmies was difficult. The results served us to refine the calculation of the germline bottleneck, and to show that children born to older mothers possess more mitochondrial variants. PNAS 2014; 111(43):15474.
  • Then, I continued working on mitochondrial genomics, but with a clinical twist. Thanks to a Chilean postdoctoral training grant (FONDECYT grant #3170280), I was able to explore the differences in the mitochondrial heteroplasmies transmission dynamics in mother-child pairs affected by 22q11.2 deletion syndrome. Genes 2021; 12(1):92 .
  • Now, and following on the previous study, our group has been funded to explore the extent of inherited mitonuclear discordance among admixed individuals affected by congenital defects, immunological conditions and cancer. We hypothesize that mitonuclear discordance affects the mitochondrial function, thus contributing to disease (FONDECYT grant #11220642).

    Collaboration: Decoding Complex Inherited Phenotypes of Rare Disorders (DECIPHERD)

    Chile lags behind in the implementation of clinical genomics. Consequently, we started a research project aimed at generating local expertise on: (1) next-generation sequencing (2) exome data analysis, and (3) providing an answer to local patients who have spent years in the health system trying to figure out the cause of their condition. The research team is a diverse group that involves clinicians, molecular biologists, bioinformaticians, and lawyers, who are very motivated to develop the field locally and contribute internationally.
    As the image shows, the patient is at the center of our efforts. Research, public policies, and clinical management, all can contribute to improving a patient's life. For more information, please read "Rare diseases in Chile: challenges and recommendations in universal health coverage context", Orphanet J Rare Dis 14, 289; and visit www.pocofrecuentes.cl.

    Bioinformatics Assistance Service

    SABio stands for "Servicio de Asistencia en Bioinformática" in Spanish, but it also translates to "wise person". We set up a cluster for high performance computing with the help of Chilean grant FONDEQUIP EQM150093. SABio, acts as a consulting service, primarily aimed at helping trainees to design and complete their genomic analyses on the cluster. For more details (in Spanish) click here.
