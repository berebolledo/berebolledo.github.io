Bio

It was my original training in molecular biology in Chile that led me to genomics and exploring ultraconserved enhancers. When transgenic frogs took over my holidays, I decided to switch to the more flexible (but no less frustrating) "dry lab", where at least I could work in pajamas from home sometimes. After receiving a Fulbright scholarship, I moved to the USA to complete my doctorate at Penn State. I worked on mitochondrial genomics (which, by the way, we should pay more attention to) under Anton Nekrutenko and Kateryna Makova's direction. Now back in Chile, I am focused on facilitating the discovery of causal variants in rare undiagnosed disorders, and clinical genomics in general. I primarily work on implementing known and novel pipelines for the analysis of sequencing data.

Decoding Complex Inherited Phenotypes of Rare Disorders (DECIPHERD)

Chile lags behind in the implementation of clinical genomics. So we started a research project aimed at generating local expertise on: (1) next-generation sequencing (2) exome data analysis, and (3) providing an answer to local patients who have spent years in the health system trying to figure out the cause of their condition. The research team is a diverse group that involves clinicians, molecular biologists, bioinformaticians, and lawyers, who are very motivated to develop the field locally and contribute internationally.
As the image shows, the patient is at the center of our efforts. Research, public policies, and clinical management, can all contribute to improving a patient's life.

Mitochondrial genomics

I spent six years of training working on a pipeline for the unequivocal identification of mtDNA heteroplasmic sites from sequencing data. It is a pipeline that now runs in less than a day, and impressively still works 5 years after publication! The results were published in 2014 in PNAS, and served to redefine the germline bottleneck, and show that children born to older mothers have more mitochondrial variants. The Makova Lab continued working on this, and recently published an updated analysis on the matter. Here in Chile, I received funding from a postdoctoral research grant (FONDECYT 3170280) to compare the mitochondrial transmission dynamic in mother-child pairs concordant and discordant for some of the 22q11.2 deletion syndrome phenotypes. A preprint of the results has been deposited in medRxiv.

Bioinformatic Analysis Service

SABio stands for "Servicio de Análisis Bioinformático" in Spanish, but it also translates as "wise person". We set up a cluster for high performance computing with the help of Chilean grant FONDEQUIP EQM150093. As such, it is a national resource available free of charge for local and external collaborators to use. SABio, thus acts as a consulting service, primarily meant to help trainees to design and complete their genomic analyses on the cluster.
