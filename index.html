Bio

I originally got trained in molecular biology in Chile, which led me to genomics and exploring ultraconserved enhancers. Transgenic frogs took over my holidays, so I decided to switch to the more flexible (but no less frustrating) "dry lab", where at least you get to work in pajamas from home sometimes. Got a Fulbright scholarship, and moved to the USA to get a doctorate at Penn State. I worked on mitochondrial genomics (which, by the way, we should pay more attention to) under Anton Nekrutenko and Kateryna Makova's direction. Now back in Chile, I am focused on facilitating the discovery of causal variants in rare undiagnosed disorders, and clinical genomics in general. I primarily work on implementing known and novel pipelines for the analysis of sequencing data.

Decoding Complex Inherited Phenotypes of Rare Disorders (DECIPHERD)

Chile is really behind in the implementation of clinical genomics. So we started a research project aimed at generating local expertise on (1) next-generation sequencing (2) exome data analysis (3) providing an answer to local patients who have spent years in the public system trying to figure out the cause of their condition. We are a diverse group of people involving clinicians, molecular biologists, bioinformaticians, and lawyers, really motivated to develop the field locally, and contribute internationally.
As the image shows, the patient is at the center of our efforts. Research, public policies and clinical management, all can contribute to improving a patient's life.

Mitochondrial genomics

I spent six years of training working on a pipeline for the unequival identification of mtDNA heteroplasmic sites from sequencing data... a pipeline that runs now in less than a day, and impressively still works 5 years after publication! The results were published back in 2014 in PNAS, and served to redefine the germline bottleneck, and show that children born to older mothers have more mitochondrial variants. The Makova Lab continued working on this, and recently published an updated analysis on the matter. Here in Chile, I got funding from a postdoc grant (FONDECYT 3170280) to compare the mitochondrial transmission dynamic in mother-child pairs concordant and discordant for some of the 22q11.2 deletion syndrome phenotypes. I am writing the manuscript, so I will be communicating the results soon.

Bioinformatic Analysis Service

SABio as an acronym means "Servicio de Análisis Bioinformático" in Spanish, but it also means "wise person" (because why not). We set up a cluster for high performance computing thanks to the Chilean grant FONDEQUIP EQM150093, and as such, it is a national resource available free of charge for local and external collaborators to use. SABio, in turn acts as a consulting instance, primarily meant to help trainees to design and complete their genomic analyses.
